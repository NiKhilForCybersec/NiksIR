Business Email Compromise (BEC)

L2 L3 Section 7.4 ~14 min read
High Financial Impact

BEC attacks caused over $2.9 billion in losses in 2023 (FBI IC3). These are high-priority incidents requiring immediate escalation to management and often Legal/HR.

What is BEC?

Business Email Compromise is a sophisticated scam targeting organizations that conduct wire transfers or handle sensitive data. Attackers impersonate executives, vendors, or partners to trick employees into transferring money or revealing information.

Traditional Phishing BEC Attack

BEC Attack Types

CEO Fraud / Executive Impersonation â–¼

Attacker poses as CEO or senior executive, requests urgent wire transfer.

Typical Scenario:

  • Email appears to come from CEO
  • Requests urgent, confidential wire transfer
  • Often targets finance department
  • "I'm in a meeting, can't talk, just do this now"

Red Flags:

  • Request for secrecy
  • Unusual urgency
  • Different reply-to address
  • Request sent outside business hours
Vendor/Supplier Compromise â–¼

Attacker compromises or impersonates vendor, requests payment to new account.

Typical Scenario:

  • Email from "vendor" about invoice
  • Claims bank account changed
  • Provides new payment details
  • Often in context of real business relationship

Red Flags:

  • Sudden bank account change request
  • Slight variation in email domain
  • Request to update payment details
  • Pressure to pay quickly
Account Compromise â–¼

Attacker gains access to legitimate email account, uses it to request payments.

Typical Scenario:

  • Employee account compromised via phishing
  • Attacker sends emails from real account
  • May create inbox rules to hide activity
  • Requests payment from contacts/vendors

Red Flags:

  • Unusual login locations/times
  • New inbox rules (forwarding, deletion)
  • Requests inconsistent with normal behavior
  • Email actually comes from legitimate address
Attorney/Legal Impersonation â–¼

Attacker poses as lawyer handling confidential matter, requests funds.

Typical Scenario:

  • Claims to represent company in legal matter
  • Requests payment for settlement/fees
  • Emphasizes confidentiality
  • Often targets finance or executives
Data Theft / W-2 Scam â–¼

Attacker impersonates executive, requests employee data (W-2s, PII).

Typical Scenario:

  • "CEO" emails HR department
  • Requests W-2 forms for all employees
  • Or requests employee directory with personal info
  • Used for tax fraud, identity theft

Peak Season:

January - April (tax season)

  • Lookalike domains (micros0ft.com)

  • Free email service for "business"

  • Request doesn't match normal behavior
  • New vendor or payment change
  • Targeting new employees

Investigation Steps

  • Analyze Email Headers
    • Check From address vs Display Name
    • Check Reply-To address
    • Verify SPF/DKIM/DMARC results
    • Examine Received headers for origin
  • Domain Analysis
    • When was domain registered? (Recent = suspicious)
    • WHOIS information - who owns it?
    • Is it similar to a legitimate domain?
  • Check for Account Compromise
    • If from internal account: check sign-in logs
    • Look for unusual locations or devices
    • Check for new inbox rules
    • Review recent sent items
  • Scope the Attack
    • Who else received similar emails?
    • Has anyone responded or taken action?
    • Were any payments made?
KQL Find similar BEC attempts in email logs
EmailEvents | where TimeGenerated > ago(7d) | where SenderFromAddress contains "suspicious-domain.com" or SenderDisplayName has_any ("CEO", "CFO", "Controller") | project TimeGenerated, SenderFromAddress, SenderDisplayName, RecipientEmailAddress, Subject, DeliveryAction | order by TimeGenerated desc
KQL Check for inbox rule creation (account compromise)
OfficeActivity | where TimeGenerated > ago(30d) | where Operation in ("New-InboxRule", "Set-InboxRule", "UpdateInboxRules") | where UserId == "[email protected]" | extend RuleDetails = tostring(Parameters) | project TimeGenerated, UserId, Operation, RuleDetails, ClientIP

Response Actions

Immediate Actions

If Payment Was Made

  1. Contact bank immediately - request recall
  2. Notify Legal and Finance leadership
  3. File FBI IC3 complaint (ic3.gov)
  4. Preserve all evidence
  5. Document timeline of events

If Caught Before Payment

  1. Block sender domain/address
  2. Search for similar emails to others
  3. Alert potential targets
  4. Document for awareness training
  5. Report to email security team for tuning

If Internal Account Compromised

  • Reset password immediately
  • Revoke all active sessions
  • Re-register MFA devices
  • Remove malicious inbox rules
  • Review and revoke app consents
  • Check sent items for attacker emails
  • Notify recipients of fraudulent emails
  • Review mailbox for data access
