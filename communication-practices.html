Communication Principles

Clear communication is essential for SOC effectiveness. Whether updating tickets, briefing management, or coordinating with other teams, how you communicate impacts response quality.

‚úì Do

  • Be clear and concise
  • State facts before opinions
  • Provide actionable information
  • Tailor to your audience
  • Document in tickets
  • Follow up proactively

‚úó Don't

  • Use jargon with non-technical people
  • Speculate without evidence
  • Blame individuals
  • Leave stakeholders guessing
  • Over-communicate irrelevant details
  • Forget to close the loop

Professional Writing

Ticket Documentation

  • Start with what, when, where
  • Include specific details (IPs, users, timestamps)
  • Explain actions taken and results
  • Note outstanding questions or blockers
  • Specify next steps clearly
  • Use consistent formatting

Good vs Bad Examples

‚ùå Poor Documentation

"Looked at alert. Seems like FP. Closed."

‚úì Good Documentation

"Investigated suspicious PowerShell alert on WORKSTATION-123.

Findings:
- Process triggered by SCCM deployment (parent: ccmexec.exe)
- Script hash matches approved IT deployment script
- User confirmed scheduled software update

Verdict: False positive - legitimate IT activity
Action: Added SCCM parent process to exclusion list"

Audience Adaptation

Audience Focus On Avoid
SOC Peers Technical details, IOCs, next steps Over-explaining basics
IT Teams What they need to do, impact Security jargon they may not know
End Users Simple explanation, what to do Technical terms, blame
Management Business impact, status, timeline Deep technical details
Executives Risk, business impact, resolution Technical details, acronyms

Translating Technical to Business

Technical Business Translation
"Detected C2 beaconing to known APT infrastructure" "Attacker has remote access to a company computer"
"Credential harvesting via LSASS dump" "Attacker is trying to steal passwords"
"Lateral movement via PsExec" "Attacker is spreading to other systems"
"Data exfiltration to cloud storage" "Company data may have been stolen"

Communication Channels

Channel Best For Not For
Ticket/Case Official record, detailed documentation Urgent real-time coordination
Chat (Slack/Teams) Quick questions, real-time coordination Critical decisions without documentation
Email Formal updates, external communication Urgent matters needing immediate response
Phone/Bridge Active incidents, complex discussions Routine updates
War Room Major incidents, multi-team coordination Normal operations
Document Everything

Verbal discussions and chat conversations should be summarized in tickets. If it's not documented, it didn't happen for purposes of handoff, metrics, and lessons learned.

Key Takeaways
  • Be clear, concise, and factual
  • Adapt communication to your audience
  • Document everything in tickets
  • Translate technical to business for non-technical audiences
  • Use the right channel for the situation
  • Follow up and close the loop
