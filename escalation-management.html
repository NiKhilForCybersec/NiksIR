Priority/Severity Definitions

Consistent priority classification ensures incidents receive appropriate attention and resources.

Priority Name Criteria Examples
P1 Critical
  • Active breach in progress
  • Confirmed ransomware
  • Data exfiltration occurring
  • Critical systems compromised
  • Multiple systems affected
 Active ransomware encryption, DC compromise, confirmed APT activity, data breach with PII exposure
P2 High
  • Confirmed malware/compromise
  • Active C2 communication
  • Privileged account compromise
  • Single critical system affected
 Confirmed malware execution, successful phishing with credential theft, lateral movement detected, admin account takeover
P3 Medium
  • Suspicious activity detected
  • Potential threat (unconfirmed)
  • Policy violations
  • Single non-critical system
 Suspicious login patterns, blocked malware, phishing email received (no click), DLP policy violation, unauthorized software
P4 Low
  • Informational alerts
  • Minor policy violations
  • Low-risk activity
  • Routine security events
 Single failed login, PUA detected and blocked, minor configuration drift, informational security advisory
Priority Escalation Triggers

Increase priority when:

  • Scope expands (more hosts/users affected)
  • High-value target involved (executive, admin, sensitive system)
  • Data exposure confirmed
  • Attack progresses to later kill chain stage
  • Media/regulatory attention possible

Escalation Criteria by Tier

L1 ‚Üí L2 Escalation ‚ñº

Escalate when:

  • Alert requires investigation beyond documented runbooks
  • Unable to make determination within 30 minutes
  • Confirmed true positive requiring response actions
  • Multiple related alerts indicating potential incident
  • High-value target involved (VIP, admin, critical server)
  • Containment actions needed (isolation, blocking)

Include in escalation:

  • Alert details and timestamp
  • Investigation steps already taken
  • Current findings and assessment
  • Specific question or action needed from L2
L2 ‚Üí L3/IR Escalation ‚ñº

Escalate when:

  • Confirmed significant security incident
  • Evidence of advanced threat actor / APT
  • Malware analysis or forensics required
  • Multiple systems compromised
  • Data breach suspected or confirmed
  • Novel attack technique not covered by procedures
  • Executive or board-level visibility needed
‚Üí Management Escalation ‚ñº

Escalate to SOC Manager/CISO when:

  • P1 Critical incident declared
  • Data breach with notification requirements
  • Ransomware with business impact
  • Media attention likely or occurring
  • Resource needs exceed normal capacity
  • Third-party notification required
  • Law enforcement engagement considered

External Stakeholder Escalation

Some incidents require engagement with teams outside the SOC.

Stakeholder When to Engage Contact Method
Legal / General Counsel Data breach, regulatory notification, law enforcement, litigation hold Phone call for urgent, email for planning
Human Resources Insider threat, employee policy violation, termination-related Phone/meeting with confidentiality
Communications / PR Public-facing incident, media inquiries, customer notification Coordinate through management
IT Operations System restoration, network changes, emergency patching Direct contact, change management for non-emergency
Business Unit Leaders Impact to their systems, approval for containment actions Through management or direct as authorized
Third-Party IR Firm Overwhelming incident, specialized expertise needed Pre-negotiated retainer, management approval
Law Enforcement Criminal activity, when required by law, management decision Through Legal, management approval required
Contact List Maintenance

Maintain an up-to-date contact list with primary and backup contacts for each stakeholder group. Include phone numbers for after-hours escalation. Review quarterly.

After-Hours Escalation

On-Call Procedures

  1. Initial Triage: On-call analyst performs initial assessment
  2. Severity Determination: Classify priority based on standard criteria
  3. P1/P2 Response: Immediate action and escalation as needed
  4. P3/P4 Response: Document and queue for next business day unless criteria escalates
  5. Management Notification: P1 incidents always notify on-call manager

Wake Management For:

  • Active ransomware
  • Confirmed data breach
  • Critical system compromise
  • Widespread attack
  • Media/regulatory impact

Can Wait Until Morning:

  • Contained single-host malware
  • Blocked phishing (no clicks)
  • Policy violations (no immediate risk)
  • Informational alerts
  • Known FP patterns

Key Takeaways
  • Use consistent priority definitions across all incidents
  • Know the SLA targets and escalation timeframes
  • Escalate early rather than late when in doubt
  • Include complete context in escalation communications
  • After-hours escalation should err on the side of caution for P1/P2
