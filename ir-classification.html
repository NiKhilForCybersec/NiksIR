Why Classification Matters

Proper classification ensures incidents receive appropriate resources, response times, and management attention. It drives SLAs, escalation paths, and reporting requirements.

Resource Allocation

Critical incidents get immediate full-team response; low-severity can wait.

SLA Compliance

Response and resolution times tied to severity levels.

Communication

Determines who gets notifiedâ€”team lead, CISO, or board.

Metrics

Enables meaningful reporting and trend analysis.

Severity Levels

Level Description Response Time Resolution Target
P1 Critical Active breach, ransomware, widespread impact, C2 detected Immediate (<15 min) <4 hours
P2 High Confirmed compromise, malware execution, data exfiltration attempt <30 minutes <24 hours
P3 Medium Suspicious activity, policy violation, potential threat <2 hours <72 hours
P4 Low Minor policy violation, informational, false positive tuning <8 hours <1 week

Severity Decision Factors

Impact â–¼
  • Scope: How many systems/users affected?
  • Data: What data is at risk? (PII, financial, IP)
  • Business: Which business functions impacted?
  • Regulatory: Are compliance requirements triggered?
Urgency â–¼
  • Active threat: Is the attack ongoing?
  • Spreading: Is it propagating to other systems?
  • Time-sensitive: Will delay increase damage?
  • Attacker presence: Is attacker currently active?
Asset Criticality â–¼
  • Crown jewels: Domain controllers, financial systems, customer data
  • Executive systems: C-suite, board members
  • Production: Revenue-generating systems
  • Standard: Regular workstations, test systems
When in Doubt, Escalate

If you're unsure whether something is P1 or P2, treat it as P1 initially. It's easier to downgrade than to recover from a delayed response.

Incident Categories

Category Description Examples
Malware Malicious software execution Ransomware, trojan, worm, cryptominer
Phishing Social engineering via email Credential phishing, BEC, spear phishing
Account Compromise Unauthorized account access Credential theft, session hijacking
Unauthorized Access Access to systems/data without permission Privilege escalation, lateral movement
Data Breach Unauthorized data access or exfiltration Data theft, accidental exposure
Denial of Service Availability attack DDoS, resource exhaustion
Insider Threat Malicious or negligent insider Data theft, sabotage, policy violation
Policy Violation Security policy breach Unauthorized software, data handling
Reconnaissance Information gathering Port scanning, enumeration
Web Attack Application-layer attacks SQLi, XSS, API abuse

Alert Verdicts

How to classify the outcome of alert investigation:

Verdict Abbr Definition Example
True Positive TP Alert correctly identified malicious activity Malware detected and confirmed
Benign True Positive BTP Alert fired correctly but activity was authorized Pentest, authorized tool, IT activity
False Positive FP Alert fired incorrectly; no malicious activity Legitimate app flagged as malware
Inconclusive INC Insufficient evidence to determine Logs missing, host unavailable

Verdict Decision Tree

  1. Was the alert detection logic correct?
    • No â†’ False Positive
    • Yes â†’ Continue
  2. Was the activity malicious?
    • Yes â†’ True Positive

  3. Could you determine either way?

Escalation Thresholds

Trigger Escalate To Timeframe
Any P1 incident SOC Lead, IR Team, Security Manager Immediately
Confirmed data breach CISO, Legal, Privacy Officer <1 hour
Ransomware confirmed CISO, Executive team, IR retainer Immediately
Domain controller compromise IR Team, AD Team, Management Immediately
Executive account compromise CISO, HR, Legal <30 min
P2 not contained in 2 hours SOC Lead, Security Manager At threshold
Insider threat suspected HR, Legal, Security Manager Before investigation
Regulatory data involved Legal, Compliance, Privacy <1 hour
Document Escalations

Always document when you escalated, to whom, and what information was provided. This creates an audit trail and ensures continuity.

Key Takeaways
  • Classification drives response speed, resources, and communication
  • Consider impact, urgency, and asset criticality
  • When uncertain, escalate up rather than down
  • Use consistent verdict terminology (TP, BTP, FP, INC)
  • Know your escalation thresholds and contacts
  • Document all classification decisions
