Communication Principles

Effective incident communication keeps stakeholders informed, maintains trust, and ensures coordinated response. Poor communication causes confusion, delays, and reputational damage.

‚úì Do

  • Communicate early and often
  • Be factual‚Äîstate what you know
  • Acknowledge what you don't know
  • Provide clear next steps
  • Tailor message to audience
  • Document all communications

‚úó Don't

  • Speculate or guess
  • Assign blame during incident
  • Over-promise on timelines
  • Use technical jargon with execs
  • Go silent during investigation
  • Communicate inconsistent messages
The Golden Rule

No news feels like bad news. Even if you have no new information, communicate that you're still investigating. Silence breeds anxiety and speculation.

Update Cadence

Severity Update Frequency Format
P1 Critical Every 30-60 minutes during active response War room + written updates
P2 High Every 2-4 hours Email/Slack + bridge call if needed
P3 Medium Daily or when significant progress Email/ticket updates
P4 Low Upon resolution or weekly Ticket updates
Even When Nothing Changes

For P1/P2 incidents, send updates even if there's no new information: "Investigation continues. No significant new findings in last hour. Next update at [time]." This prevents stakeholders from wondering if you've forgotten about them.

Executive Briefings

Executive Summary Format

Executives need concise, business-focused information. Use the BLUF (Bottom Line Up Front) approach.

Executive Briefing Structure

  1. Bottom Line (1-2 sentences) - What happened and current status
  2. Business Impact - How this affects operations, customers, revenue
  3. Scope - What/who is affected
  4. Response Status - What we're doing, contained or not
  5. Risk Level - Current and trending
  6. Timeline - When we expect resolution
  7. Decisions Needed - If any from leadership

What NOT to Include for Executives

  • Technical details (IOCs, CVE numbers, attack techniques)
  • Tool names and log sources
  • Internal team discussions
  • Speculation or theories
  • Blame or finger-pointing

External Communication

External Communication Approval

All external communication during incidents must be approved by Legal and Communications/PR. SOC analysts should never communicate externally without explicit authorization.

  • Tailor message to audience‚Äîexecutives need business impact, not technical details
