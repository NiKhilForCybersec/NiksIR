Event vs Incident

Understanding the distinction between events and incidents is fundamental to IR.

Security Event

Any observable occurrence in a system or network.

  • User login
  • File access
  • Firewall connection
  • Email received

Most events are normal business activity.

Security Incident

A violation or imminent threat of violation of security policies or practices.

  • Malware infection
  • Unauthorized access
  • Data breach
  • Denial of service

Incidents require formal response.

1. Preparation â–¼

Build the capability to respond effectively before incidents occur.

  • Develop and maintain IR plan and playbooks
  • Build and train IR team
  • Deploy detection and response tools
  • Establish communication channels and contacts
  • Conduct exercises and tabletops
  • Ensure logging and visibility

Preparation is ongoingâ€”not just a one-time effort.

2. Detection & Analysis â–¼

Identify potential incidents and determine their scope and impact.

  • Monitor alerts from security tools
  • Receive reports from users and external parties
  • Analyze and validate potential incidents
  • Determine scope, impact, and priority
  • Document initial findings
  • Notify appropriate stakeholders

This is where SOC analysts spend most of their time.

3. Containment, Eradication & Recovery â–¼

Stop the incident, remove the threat, and restore operations.

Containment

  • Short-term: Immediate actions to stop damage (isolate host, block IP)
  • Long-term: More sustainable controls while preparing remediation

Eradication

  • Remove malware and attacker access
  • Eliminate persistence mechanisms
  • Patch exploited vulnerabilities

Recovery

  • Restore systems to normal operation
  • Validate systems are clean
  • Monitor for recurrence
4. Post-Incident Activity â–¼

Learn from the incident to improve future response.

  • Conduct lessons learned meeting
  • Create post-incident report
  • Identify improvement opportunities
  • Update playbooks and procedures
  • Implement preventive measures
  • Share relevant intelligence

Often skipped due to time pressureâ€”don't skip it!

IR Team Roles

Effective incident response requires clear roles and responsibilities.

Role Responsibilities Typical Title
Incident Commander Overall coordination, decision authority, stakeholder communication IR Manager, SOC Manager, CISO
Lead Analyst Technical investigation lead, coordinate analysis tasks Senior Analyst, IR Lead
Analysts Investigation, log analysis, evidence collection SOC Analyst, IR Analyst
Forensics Deep forensic analysis, malware analysis, evidence preservation Forensic Analyst, Malware Analyst
IT Support System access, containment actions, restoration System Admin, Network Admin
Communications Internal/external communications, documentation Comms Lead, Scribe
Legal/Compliance Regulatory guidance, notification requirements, law enforcement General Counsel, Privacy Officer
For Smaller Teams

Not every organization has dedicated IR staff. SOC analysts often fill multiple roles. The key is having clear ownershipâ€”someone must be in charge of each function, even if one person wears multiple hats.

When to Declare an Incident

Not every alert becomes an incident. Use these criteria to determine when formal incident declaration is warranted.

Declare an Incident When:
  • Confirmed malware execution on systems
  • Confirmed unauthorized access to systems or data
  • Evidence of data exfiltration or theft
  • Active attack in progress
  • Compromise of privileged accounts
  • Multiple systems or users affected
  • Regulatory notification may be required
  • Potential business impact exceeds threshold
Likely NOT an Incident:
  • Single blocked malware (no execution)
  • Phishing email reported but no user interaction
  • Failed attack attempts (blocked by controls)
  • Minor policy violations with no security impact
  • False positives after investigation
When in Doubt

If you're unsure whether to declare an incident, escalate to your lead or manager. It's better to declare and downgrade than to miss a real incident. You can always close an incident as "no impact" after investigation confirms it's benign.
