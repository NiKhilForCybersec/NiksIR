SLA Overview

Service Level Agreements (SLAs) define expected performance standards. For SOC, SLAs typically cover how quickly we respond to and resolve security incidents.

Response Time SLA

How quickly you acknowledge and begin working on an alert or incident.

Clock starts: When alert is generated or ticket created

Clock stops: When analyst begins investigation

Resolution Time SLA

How quickly the incident is fully resolved or closed.

Clock starts: When ticket is created

Clock stops: When ticket is resolved/closed

Business Hours vs 24x7

SLAs may be measured in business hours (8am-5pm M-F) or calendar hours (24x7). Critical incidents are typically 24x7 while lower priorities may be business hours only.

Key Metrics

Metric Definition Why It Matters
MTTA Mean Time to Acknowledge How fast do we start investigating?
MTTR Mean Time to Respond/Resolve How fast do we fix the problem?
MTTD Mean Time to Detect How fast do we find threats?
MTTC Mean Time to Contain How fast do we stop the spread?
SLA Compliance % % of tickets meeting SLA Overall performance measurement

Typical SLA Targets

Priority Response Time Resolution Time Hours
P1 Critical 15 minutes 4 hours 24x7
P2 High 30 minutes 8 hours 24x7
P3 Medium 2 hours 24 hours Business hours
P4 Low 8 hours 72 hours Business hours
Your SLAs May Vary

These are typical targets. Your organization's SLAs may be different based on industry, regulatory requirements, and contractual obligations. Always know your specific SLAs.

Meeting SLAs

Best Practices

Prioritize Effectively â–¼
  • Work on highest priority first
  • Don't let P3/P4 distract from P1/P2
  • Re-prioritize as new information emerges
Monitor Queue Continuously â–¼
  • Check queue regularly (every 15-30 min)
  • Use dashboards showing SLA status
  • Set up alerts for approaching breaches
Acknowledge Quickly â–¼
  • Acknowledge alerts immediately when you start
  • This stops the response time clock
  • Even if full investigation takes time
Escalate Early â–¼
  • Don't wait until SLA is about to breach
  • If you're stuck, escalate sooner
  • Better to escalate early than miss SLA
Document Progress â–¼
  • Update tickets with progress regularly
  • Note blockers or dependencies
  • Helps justify SLA exceptions if needed

Queue Management Tips

  • Process new alerts within first 15 minutes of shift
  • Check SLA timers before starting new work
  • Handle "quick wins" to reduce queue volume
  • Communicate with team about high-priority items
  • Hand off cleanly at shift change

Handling SLA Breaches

If SLA Is About to Breach

  1. Escalate immediately to shift lead/manager
  2. Document why resolution is taking longer
  3. Identify what resources are needed
  4. Communicate status to stakeholders

If SLA Has Breached

  1. Continue working to resolution (don't stop just because breached)
  2. Document the breach circumstances
  3. Notify manager per process
  4. After resolution, identify root cause of breach

Common Breach Reasons

Reason Prevention
Queue overload Alert on queue depth, call in backup
Complex investigation Escalate earlier, parallel work
Waiting on external team Track dependencies, follow up proactively
Incorrect priority Re-classify appropriately
Shift handoff gaps Better handoff documentation
Key Takeaways
  • Know your organization's specific SLA targets
  • Acknowledge alerts quickly to stop response clock
  • Prioritize P1/P2 over lower priorities
  • Escalate early if you might miss SLA
  • Document progress and blockers
  • If SLA breaches, keep working and document why
  • SLA compliance is a team metric, not just individual
